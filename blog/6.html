I originally wrote the song “Call Me” on an unamplified electric guitar in my tiny studio apartment in San Francisco towards the end of my co-op. Driven largely by a i-i-i-iv progression, it’s one of the simplest songs I’ve written, musically-speaking; however, lyrically, I believe it is one of my most revealing tracks to date.

When I write, I generally don’t put on any sort of persona or seek to shock à la Marilyn Manson [1]; nor do I attempt to tell much of a story beyond what is going on in my head at the time. My lyrics tend to be self-reflective and introspective, in that I use them as a means of therapy. When my words do take on a meaning beyond the initially apparent, it’s only as an allegory for those emotions. In that sense, I don’t write for an audience, or necessarily aim to help others with my work like Lady Gaga does [2] - I write as a form of self-therapy, and simply hope that anyone who enjoys my music is able to find relief in it in much the same way that I do.

With that said, I wrote this song to myself - in that the song’s lyrics are about, and addressed to myself. Although the lyrics are in second person, this was a purposeful decision to essentially try to dissociate myself from what was troubling me. At the latter end of my time in San Francisco, I was struggling with depression due to having felt that I largely wasted my time there. I spent half of the six months I’d lived in San Francisco either suffering from or recovering from Lyme disease, which led to me living a largely shut-in life outside of work - having fallen into a too ordinary routine and living alone in the bad part of town took a large mental toll on me, which led to the creation of this song.

In the lyrics, I address essentially three parts of myself as lost - my bones, my brain, and my heart. Bones had more to do with my physical state while I was sick - as I was suffering from Lyme disease, each time I moved it essentially felt as if my bones had splintered and stuck directly into my muscles, making every movement agonizing. As it took me about a month or two to even be diagnosed with the disease due to its rarity on the west coast, I had become well-accustomed to this constant pain, having had it ascribed to a lack of stretching and physical activity by doctors. Thus, I decided to dissociate from my physical body while writing this, essentially taking on the role of a ghost to put that pain behind me - at this point, I had already completed my course of antibiotics, but the lingering feelings of regret at having missed out on so much due to my illness stayed.

The “brain” verse of the lyrics was referring to a more mental struggle - I’m not sure if I can be clinically diagnosed with anything, but I often find myself dealing with a generally low self-esteem, moodiness, and poor mentality. Combined with being a shut-in whose only notable social interactions occurred during work or online for several months, while generally needing more social contact than that in order to be able to vent my feelings in a more healthy way, I found myself at a definite low point mentally-speaking towards the end of my time in San Francisco. This section of the song was written largely as a stream of consciousness, as I tried to describe the complete standstill my thoughts often got stuck in, or the pounding repetition of my distaste for myself.

Finally, the last verse was meant to be a slightly quieter, more grounding one - one that stemmed from my loneliness. I had expected that while I’m in San Francisco, being three thousand miles away from my friends and family, that I’d find at least a short term relationship while un-weighed down by responsibilities to my loved ones. That did not happen, at least in part due to being hampered by that illness. As such, this ended up being the most sincere verse, in that it accurately reflects my crestfallen state of mind at my failure to find a relationship. Towards the end of the verse, however, the lyrics take a sharp left turn and refocus on myself as a whole.

Essentially, due to knowing that no matter how bad my mental state gets, I will never allow myself to actively self-harm or attempt suicide, I’ve managed to stick to a mantra of “It’ll be alright.” This mantra repeats in the lyrics ad infinitum until it turns to doubt, (“It’ll be/Will it be?”) and a final rejection (“No, no, no!”) of its meaning.

By allowing things to just be “alright,” one denies themselves both the peaks and valleys of life - I had essentially numbed myself to the situation I was in, and wasn’t doing anything to make it better. As I’d mentioned earlier, by the time I wrote “Call Me,” several months had passed since I had finally found treatment for my illness - and yet, I’d allowed myself to linger in the same shut-in state that I had been while I was sick, despite having recovered already. By rejecting the mantra of “it’ll be alright,” I decided to bear responsibility for myself and my mental state once again in an effort to avoid making the same mistakes in the future, and give myself a proverbial kick in the ass for allowing myself to get to this state.

Musically, this piece reflects my background rather clearly. My musical influences span a wide variety of genres, from J-Pop, to emo, to blues, to death metal. Being familiar with the latter genre, I’m no stranger to a rapid escalation of intensity; being familiar with the former, I have a similar amount of exposure to the power of repetition. I essentially ended up taking a blues-inspired verse and chord structure, writing some admittedly emo lyrics to said structure, then escalating the ending to a near-screaming pitch. The song is driven far more by its lyrics than its music, owing to my initial exposure to music being Russian folk, where the music is completely secondary to the lyrics.

I recorded this on Sunday, March 24 with my writing partner and bandmate, Matthew Garrell. Our voices are dubbed together. I played guitar and bass, and we arranged the piano and drums together. The original composition didn’t include piano, but we recognized that the inclusion of a repeating piano riff in the climax of the song and a brief piano interlude in the middle added more character - the repeating riff aided in creating a panicked feeling, owing to the general loudness of the section, and the short solo helps ingrain the vocal melody.
