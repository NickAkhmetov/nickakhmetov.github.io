Interview Notes/Transcript

Death metal is a finely tuned, lethal form of art. Given the complexity of the genre, a peek behind the curtain of its creation offers an enlightening glance into both the genre and its purveyors.

Frontman Dan Wilson and rhythm guitarist Mike Gauthier of local symphonic death metal band Vivisepulture were kind enough to allow me to conduct a fairly in-depth ethnographic interview, in which we discussed their creative process, their history and involvement in metal, and their identity as artists.

The band was founded by Mike when he was still in high school. He and Dan first met approximately a year and a half later, when they played a show together while Dan was the frontman of another group. Several iterations of the band’s lineup later, they released their debut album, “Omen,” and have built their following to almost 2,000 Facebook likes, and have opened for bands such as melodic death metal giants Dark Tranquillity and Insomnium. The band follow mythological themes in their lyrics, from the Greek myth of Medusa in “To Stone,” to the Egyptian God Anubis in “The King of Restless Souls.”

Despite the musicians’ heavy involvement in the band, neither of them believes that they are necessarily reflected in the work they produce. Mike defines metal as “a big part of [his] life,” but that it doesn’t define him as a person off of the stage. Outside of his involvement in metal, Mike is a game design major at Fitchburg State University, and self-describes as an “introvert, big nerd” and as someone who doesn’t “want to leave the house very often.” He describes metal as “one of many masks” that he wears in his life, echoing the words of James Nesbitt by conveying the idea that “we all have different masks we put on for different occasions” [1]. Dan confirms his cowriter’s thoughts, adding that if he were to write something that better reflected himself as a person, it would be something more mellow and “a little bit more spacey.” While he does want to incorporate more emotional impact into their work, that’s not a priority, just a personal interest. He sees the music they produce as a lot of fun to play, but that it doesn’t define their lives - with that said, he mentioned that he has listened to the works of people who do invest their entire hearts into their work, and that “you can hear the emotion they’ve poured into it” - however, as Mike aptly states, Vivisepulture “don’t know how to write emotions - they know how to write in Lydian [mode].” The band's approach to music is perhaps a sharp contrast to the likes of Billie Holiday, whose writing conveyed her emotional and physical state so clearly that her voice needed to be sped up by 1/4 pitch to avoid sounding too depressing [2].

Mike and Dan both cite being on the stage, or being a representative of the band as being very different from their everyday life. Dan credits his involvement in the local metal scene as being instrumental in making him “more social” and better at “managing his mind” in social scenarios, despite him self-describing as an introvert. When the band was given the opportunity to set up their merchandise in a more populated area of the venue during one of the largest shows they played, Dan seized the moment - despite needing all day next day to recover from the experience. His next challenge is “managing to be the face of the band, but knowing [his] limit.” Mike also credits his involvement in music at least partially for his increased patience and communication skills, but echoes Dan’s concerns regarding the difficulty of networking - while in his everyday life, he “shake[s] hands with every person [he] meet[s], [he] talk[s] to them about their interests, [his] interests, but [he] just cannot network at shows.” Though the pair have improved their communications, the difficulty of networking can perhaps be chalked up to the divide which exists between performers and their audiences in western music, as described by Thomas Turino [3]. Due to the existence of this divide, and essentially being elevated to the status of people admiring you, networking immediately becomes more difficult, and this barrier is difficult to overcome for both performers and their audience.

Following this personal reflection, the conversation pivoted to a discussion of the music itself. Dan and Mike concurred that there was a serious dearth of high quality symphonic metal out there, a few of their personal inspirations (Septiflesh, Fleshgod Apocalypse) aside. Most symphonic bands have a “very campy, very happy” sound - in our conversation, we likened this sound to something along the lines of Lord of the Rings, while Mike said that in that sort of cross-media comparison, he would hearken Vivisepulture’s output to be along the lines of Bloodborne. The band’s overall aim is to not only fill this gap, but to make sure that the symphonic aspect is heavily tied into the overall sound - in Dan’s words, they want to “express the reason” why they are specifically a symphonic death metal band, as opposed to simply a death metal band. This also ties in heavily with their overall image, i.e. their use of formal attire on stage - Mike specified that their stagewear is purposeful, in that it both pays respect to the symphonic roots of their work, and that it is “aesthetically pleasing” to have this image ready in the event that they do play with an actual orchestra, rather than regularly dressing like “street rats.” Dan and Mike both mentioned that this choice of dress also catches the eyes of their audience and aids in band image recognition.

Mike saw their last album, Omen, as lacking consistency due to being written over the course of three different lineups of Vivisepulture, making their primary goal for their next record “consistency.” He also noted difficulty in “securing orchestration” during the production of Omen, which led to some of the symphonic death metal group’s songs entirely lacking their symphonic element, which was “discouraging.” However, the fact stands that this album is now a low-water mark for any future productions, i.e., in the words of Dan, “this is the lowest [they] can achieve for [their] next goal.” Despite this self-critique, the point stands that this album has been well-received by a majority of their audience - and as Mike said, the artist often offers themselves the harshest critique.

Both Dan and Mike agree that any future production will be superior to Omen from start to finish, part of which will stem from the creative process they’re following. While Dan doesn’t currently play an instrument, he and Mike are key in making sure the overall “theme” of each song will be well-reflected in its music. While they are still currently experimenting with their sound given their current lineup, they have already finished writing a few songs for the next record, with heavy input - and “arguing” - from each member of the band. Everybody has strong opinions, “everybody knows what they want,” and there is a certain “balancing act” that they must follow in order for everyone to be happy while producing a cohesive product. They plan to continue their mythology-based writing, citing Rick Riordan’s “Percy Jackson” series as another contemporary work which extends and modifies ancient myths passed down through oral tradition.

Vivisepulture have an upcoming local concert on March 24th at Wonder Bar in Allston, and are opening for Fleshgod Apocalypse and Hypocrisy on March 30th at the Palladium in Worcester.

Their debut album, Omen is available for streaming on Bandcamp and Spotify; it is also available for purchase or download on Bandcamp.
