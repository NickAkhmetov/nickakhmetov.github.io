A Cinematic Mistake

How to Muck Up A High Fantasy Adaptation
2019 | March 9

Word Count: 1550

Author's Note:

For the purpose of full disclosure, I have not exposed myself to this media within the past several years. However, I feel that the works I am describing have left a strong enough impression on me for me to still be able to comment on them. To aid in my recollection of events in the two adaptations contrasted, I used the WikiSummaries page as a reference regarding the events in the novel, and several online resources to jog my memory regarding the contents of the movie, including The Agony Booth's takedown. In the interest of brevity, I did not touch upon the mishandling of Murtagh's character, the absolute lack of consequences from the final battle of Durza, and the overall stupidity of including/involving Galbatorix in any way shape or form in the first movie.

I would also invite any readers to reference the rather thorough page on the Inheritance Wiki to witness exactly how much of the details of the world of Eragon the movie's creators got wrong.

Christopher Paolini’s Eragon is one of many young adult high fantasy novels. The first entry in the Inheritance Cycle, the book was published in 2004, when the author was only 15 years old; it was immediately slated for a movie adaptation with well-regarded actors such as Rachel Weiss, John Malkovich, Jeremy Irons, and Robert Carlyle attached to the project. The silver screen adaptation, though a commercial success, was immediately branded as a critical failure, which presented a “world without much texture of depth [1]” and now sits with a 16% approval rating on review aggregation site RottenTomatoes [2]. This serves as a stark contrast to the reception to the book, which critics acclaimed for “wrestl[ing] the ponderous clichés [of high fantasy] into a clear and forceful narrative. [3]” The delta in critical appraisal of the work stems largely from the media’s presentation of Christopher Paolini’s world, wherein the written form excels at drawing a clear image, while the movie fails to present an impressive window into the same world.

The World

The first major misstep in the cinematic adaptation of Eragon is its downscaling of Paolini’s imaginary world, Alagaesia, and the downscaling of the map its creator had made. In the third chapter of Mark J. P. Wolf’s Building Imaginary Worlds, the author posits the following in chapter 3:

"Maps relate a series of locations to each other, visually unifying them into a world. They provide a concrete image of a world, and fill in many of the gaps not covered in the story; gaps between locations, at the world’s edges, and places not otherwise mentioned or visited by the characters." [4]

Mark J.P. Wolf, Building Imaginary Worlds: The Theory and History of Subcreation Chapter 3

However, the creators of the movie demonstrably failed to pay proper respect to the map of the source material, either by downscaling it and failing to show the passage of time, or by carving it up to better suit the film’s runtime. The book dedicated multiple chapters to the party travelling between major destinations, with major events occurring in between, as well as discussions of areas of the world which would be visited later in the series. The movie minimized these segments, removing the sense of scale Alagaesia had in the books while making it feel absolutely empty. By omitting these conversations from the movie for the sake of not padding it for a sequel which never happened, the movie’s creators did a disservice to the original material. Additionally, the combination of two pivotal locations in the story (Gil’ead and Dras-Leona) into one amalgam does a disservice to the source material by further shrinking the map and disregarding the purpose of both cities.

The Backstory

As with any high fantasy, in order to have any sort of vested interest in Eragon’s story, there’s a certain amount of context which is necessary to keep in mind. Wolf specifies that any “stories set in secondary [i.e. imaginary] worlds may need to rely on backstory” due to the audience not being familiar with these worlds and the conflicts inherent [4]. Eragon’s backstory and revelation thereof is key in involving the reader in the conflict presented in the Inheritance Cycle.

In the book, the backstory of Alagaesia is revealed through candid conversations and various small details visible as minor occurrences - the presence of imperial troops, Brom’s concealment of his rider’s mark, and various conversations between Eragon and Brom before and after their relationship as student and mentor is established. While the general gist of the backstory of the world is kept intact, its means of delivery in the movie is heavyhanded - either by way of a long opening narration, or poorly-written exposition-dump dialogue which - unlike the book - is not interspersed with more organic worldbuilding by means of demonstrating everyday life under the reign of the empire. As a specific contrast, in the book, the rebels that Eragon joins are talked about in hushed whispers by the townspeople, while in the movie, Brom immediately reveals their existence and his goal of guiding Eragon to them. This not only fails to present the backstory in an effective manner, it detracts from the film itself by poorly translating the elements necessary to become invested in the narrative.

The Narrative

The overarching narrative of the book was also butchered to better fit the silver screen. Wolf places the following importance on narrative in chapter 4 of his book:

"Narrative is by far the most common structure found in imaginary worlds, and the reason that most of them exist in the first place." [4]

Mark J.P. Wolf, Building Imaginary Worlds: The Theory and History of Subcreation Chapter 4

The cinematic adaptation warps the scale of time of the original tale, and makes modifications to the overall narrative to emphasize the algeady glaring parallels between its source material and Tolkien’s Lord of the Rings. In the book, the bond between Eragon and his dragon, Saphira, was established over a significant portion of the book - the boy was depicted as taking care of the dragon Saphira and raising her in secret, appropriately enough for a world where dragons were outlawed. In the movie, Saphira almost immediately becomes a full-fledged adult with very little emphasis on the length of the process. This not only directly removes the section of the book that got the most critical praise [3] but makes the bond between them seem absolutely unnatural

The changes made to the role played by the ra’zac are particularly grievous. Eragon’s departure from his childhood home, Carvahall, is triggered by the grievous slaying of his uncle, Garrow, by a pair of bird-like humanoid assassins, the ra’zac. While these assassins are a fairly obvious expy of Tolkien’s Nazgûl, the movie not only needlessly changes their biology to be insectoid in form, but has the main character succeed in his initial goal of slaying them. This leads to the complete severance of the plot thread of their looming threat eventually leading to all of Carvahall rising up against the Empire. Additionally, in the book, the loss of Brom as a consequence of a ra’zac ambush is a lot more painful, due to nothing whatsoever being gained from the encounter, as opposed to the handling of this encounter in the movie, where Brom survives, and is later killed off during the rescue of another character.

Another disservice to the book is observed in the movie’s handling of the urgal race. While the book shows urgals and their greater brethren, the kull, as their own separate race, the movie goes out of its way to show their creation by one of the main villains of the work, Durza the Shade, echoing the creation of the Uruk-hai by Saruman in Tolkien’s work, and detracting from the narrative of Paolini’s work by downplaying the loss of agency of the urgals, which is addressed in the second book in the series.

The Window

The final and primary issue that comes with an adaptation is how well a story fits in the window allotted by the medium. Wolf defines a “window” as a view into an imaginary world in Chapter 1 [4]. In the case of backstory-heavy high fantasy, a cinematic adaptation falls short in its presentation in most scenarios simply because the intricacies of its world cannot often be conveyed in the limited time offered by a single movie.

In the case of Peter Jackson’s adaptation of Lord of the Rings, the director was able to work with three movies’ worth of screentime to present the full extent of the world he was adapting, with a grand total runtime of 9.2 hours for the theatrical cut, and 11.4 hours for the extended cut [5] to adapt 1241 paperback pages [6], covering approximately 135 pages per hour in the theatrical cut. Conversely, Eragon’s movie covers 503 pages of material [7] in just 103 minutes of runtime [2] for a count of 300 pages per hour. While this is by no means a complete correlation, in order to be able to adapt any semblance of the story, the screenwriters had to cut out a whole city (Teirm), prematurely sever an entire plot thread (the Ra’zac), combine two cities into one (Gil’ead and Dras Leona), downplay an entire character’s role (Angela) and otherwise change the entire pacing of what is meant to only be the first act in a series of books. Had the screenwriters faithfully followed the pace of the book, the movie’s duration would have been extended, and its world would not have felt nearly as textureless and bland.

More recently, HBO’s Game of Thrones adaptation has demonstrated that a TV show may be a more appropriate medium for high fantasy, as the serialized format allows for a higher total runtime and for parallel presentation of multiple plot threads, and thus affording the imaginary world a more appropriate window. By the using the previous metric of pages per hour of screentime, the first season of the show was 557 minutes long [8], and the book which it adapted was 848 pages long [9], for a page per hour count of approximately 91 pages per hour; the first season was consequently lauded for its faithfulness and accuracy to the source material [10].

In short...

The critical failure of Eragon singlehandedly stymied the young adult high fantasy genre in the mid-2000’s. The biggest shortcomings of the adaptation lie in its disservice to Paolini’s world and the creative liberties taken in condensing its plot to a 103-minute runtime. The adaptation clearly demonstrates the importance of heeding the original source material’s strengths, and accentuating them, rather than stripping them away to expose weaknesses.
